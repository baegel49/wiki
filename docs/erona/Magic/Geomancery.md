*A note from the Seventh Sanctum's comparative catalogue of magical traditions, compiled in the 137th year of the Cenerian Age.*

Geomancery is, as best we can determine, the oldest magical tradition in the known world — predating [[Arcanum]], [[Phasonomy]], and any tradition we can trace through written record. We know this primarily because evidence of Geomancery has been found at sites we associate with the [[Titans]], which places its origins well before the Mycean Age and possibly before the Fabled Age as well. What the Titans understood about it, we have inferred with some but not complete confidence.

The practice, as it survives today, involves carving specific shapes and symbols into natural materials such as earth, stone, wood, to produce magical effects. The underlying mechanism appears related to the same principles as Arcanum: inscription imposing intention upon the world. Whether Geomancery and Arcanum share a common origin, or whether Arcanum was developed independently and happened to converge on similar principles, is a question the Sanctum has debated for generations without resolution.

Our knowledge of Geomancery is frustratingly thin. Living practitioners are rare, and those we have encountered have been reluctant to share the details of their craft. The carvings we have studied in the field are difficult to interpret without a practitioner's guidance. We suspect that significant knowledge has been lost, and that what survives is a fragment of what was once a far more developed tradition. If any scholar has access to a willing practitioner, the Sanctum would consider this a matter of considerable priority.

One area of Geomancery that survives is the Druidry of the [[Brethiln]], which combines shape carving of natural materials with potion brewing in inscribed cauldrons. Theirs, however, is a secretive tradition and make a point of refusing to teach outsiders. It is the Sanctum's goal to invite a delegation of druids to record their ways before they are lost to time.
