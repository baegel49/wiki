Compiled by Therodos in the year 151 of the Cenerian Age. 

Concerning the [[Thalen]] and the road by which they came into [[Osera]], I have gathered what is told, both by the Thalen themselves in the songs of their singers and by the [[Brethiln]] who received them at spear-point, and I set it down knowing that the accounts do not agree, and that the land they are said to have crossed is one from which few return to be questioned.

The Thalen are a people of the [[Unsung Lands]], which lie east of Osera beyond the [[Karrow|wastes]], joined to it (so the traders relate) by a barren prairie of mesas across which no settled folk will dwell. Of their manner I can speak with more confidence than of their beginnings, for they are before us now. They are horsemen above all other peoples known to me, and it is held on both shores of the [[Akkron Wall]] that the lance couched beneath the arm, and the iron loop for the foot which they name the stirrup, were brought to their perfection by Thalen hands. From the same hardship they learned the raising of walls. The elder writers say that the eastern marches of their homeland were pressed by such terrors that the Thalen became builders of strongholds out of necessity, and that it was this craft, more than their horses, which afterward gave them the mastery over the Brethiln, who built in timber and knew nothing of the high keep of stone.

As to why they departed the Unsung Lands, I have heard three accounts and cannot arbitrate among them. The first, and to my mind the likeliest, is that the [[Haze]] crept westward over their pastures and the [[Horrors]] multiplied behind it, so that the eastern marches became unlivable and the people were driven before the calamity. The second is that the prairie failed them in some season of famine. The third, which is the account their own singers prefer, is that no misfortune drove them at all, and that [[Aldric]] led them west of his own will, so that they went as a nation seeking a country and not as fugitives fleeing one. Where a people relates the tale most to its own glory, I am accustomed to trust it least.

However that may be, all accounts agree that [[Aldric]], whom the Thalen name the great king and the father of their present line, gathered the kindreds and brought them across the Karrow. Here the sober inquirer must slow his pace, for the Karrow is no country for a people to cross. It lies under the low Haze without end of season, and in the Haze the Horrors walk, and there is little water. That a whole nation with its women, its herds, and its wagons passed over such ground is a thing so improbable that certain of my predecessors have denied it outright. [[Alvan of Iphora]] held that the Thalen never crossed the Karrow at all, but came by some northern road along the skirts of the Akkron, and that the crossing was invented afterward by their singers to magnify the deed. I do not follow him in this, for the Thalen relate the passage with too many particulars, and with too much grief, for it to be wholly a song.

By what means they endured it I cannot say for certain, and here the tellings divide. The Thalen singers say they crossed in a single season, riding through the days and camping ringed by fires against the night, and that [[Aldric]] knew the high places where the Haze thins. Others, and among these are certain [[Rurii]] who claim their fathers watched the passage from afar, say the Thalen went over in the years when the Haze lay lowest, following the dry watercourses, and that they lost a great multitude upon the way, so that the number who set out from the Unsung Lands was many times the number who came down into Brethil. Of numbers I will set down none with confidence, for the Thalen count their dead in the tens of thousands and the Brethiln count them in the hundreds of thousands, and both, I think, count to serve their purpose.

Between the Karrow and the green country of [[Brethil]] stands the Akkron, that wall of mountains which the Myceans first crossed in the old days. Through some pass of the Akkron the Thalen must have come, though which pass is disputed, and whether the watch that now keeps those heights, the Dunwarden, then kept them, I cannot determine. Some say the Dunwarden were raised in part on account of this very passage, its keepers having learned that a whole people could come through where they had feared only the single Horror. In the passes the Thalen are said to have met the mountain folk, and it may be that the [[Altani]], whom the ignorant call Titans, came down into Osera in the Thalen's company or close behind them, for the Altani are now found in Brethil where before they were not. Whether they came as allies, as captives, or by a road of their own in the same years, I have not been able to learn, and I mark it here as a thing still dark.

When at last the Thalen came down into Brethil, they came as a people who had already outlasted the worst that could be set against them, and the Brethiln, who were carpenters and hunters and had made no war upon such a scale, could not hold the central woods against them. The Thalen raised their keeps of stone upon the conquered ground, and where a keep stood the country about it was theirs, and so by keep after keep they made the realm of Thalendor in the heart of Osera. Their seat is now at [[Ostellyth]], a city of the northern grainlands which came into Thalen hands at a later time and by a different war, of which I will speak elsewhere. The line of Aldric rules there to this day. Of the old Brethiln realm there remain only the three lesser realms of [[Velwynne]], [[Mirena]], and [[Lonova]], and even in these the Thalen and the Altani increase while the elder people diminish.

This much concerning the migration I am willing to set down as likely. From it, within a single lifetime, came the strife the Thalen call the [[Sons' War]], when the children of Aldric fell to quarrelling over his seat, and out of that strife rose [[Normund]], who reigns now, and who has carried Thalen arms even upon the water against the Cenerians, a thing no man would have foretold of a people bred upon the dry mesas of the east. But these are matters for another accounting. As for the crossing itself, let the reader hold it as I hold it: that a hard people came out of a hard land by a road which should have killed them, and that the songs which tell it, though they lie in the manner of all songs, do not lie in this, that the thing was terrible.
